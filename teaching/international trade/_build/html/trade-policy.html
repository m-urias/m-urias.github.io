International Trade Policy#

{Trade Restrictions} Many countries impose some type of trade restriction, usually under pressure from special interest groups that may be hurt by trade, or to exert political pressure.

We will evaluate the costs and benefits, welfare effects, and the winners and losers of tariffs.

{Trade Restrictions} There are several types of trade protections that countries use:

  • \textbf{tariffs}: a tax on imports or exports- \textbf{export subsidies}: government pays exporters- \textbf{voluntary export restraints}: exporting country agrees to limit exports (usually under pressure)- \textbf{import quotas}: limits the quantity of a specific import- \textbf{regulations/customs/licensing}: importer must obtain licenses to buy certain products/safety regulations on imports/bureaucratic rules

{Tariffs} Tariffs are the most common form of trade restriction.

  • An import tariff is a tax on the imported commodity- An export tariff is a tax on the exported commodity

Many developing countries rely on export tariffs (e.g. Ghana on its cocoa and Brazil on its coffee) to get better prices and raise revenues.

Some developed countries do not allow the use of export tariffs but do use import tariffs (e.g. the US Constitution prohibits export tariffs).

{Tariffs} Tariffs can be ad valorem, specific, or compound.

  • \textbf{Ad valorem tariffs} are taxes levied as a fraction of the value of the imported good (e.g. 40 percent Chinese tariff on imported cigarettes)- \textbf{Specific tariffs} are levied as a fixed amount for each unit of goods imported (e.g. $505 per tonne of aluminum Chinese tariff)- \textbf{Compound tariff} is a combination of an ad valorem and a specific tariff.

{Types of trade protections} China categorizes import tariffs into six groups:

  • \textbf{general rates}: tariff rates applied to imports from countries that do not have a trade agreement or MFN status with the importing country- \textbf{most-favored-mation (MFN) rates}: standard, non-discriminatory tariffs applied to all WTO members. Under the WTO principle of non-discrimination, members must extend the same MFN rate to all other members.- \textbf{agreement rates}: negotiated tariff rates that apply to imports under specific trade agreements, such as Free Trade Agreements (FTAs) or Customs Unions. They are often lower than MFN rates.

{Types of trade protections}

  • \textbf{preferential rates}: reduced or zero tariffs offered unilaterally or under special schemes to certain countries, especially developing or least-developed countries. They are not reciprocal and aim to promote trade and development.- \textbf{tariff rate quota rates}: two-tiered tariff system with a lower (in-quota) tariff rate is applied to a set quantity (quota) of imports and a higher (out-of-quota) tariff rate applies to imports exceeding the quota limit.- \textbf{provisional rates}: temporary tariff rates, often introduced while final rates are being negotiated or while a formal review or investigation is ongoing (e.g., an anti-dumping or safeguard investigation).

{Types of trade protections} The dutiable value of an imported good is its cost, insurance, and freight (CIF) price, which includes the normal transaction price of the good, plus the cost of packing, freight, insurance, and seller’s commission. China Customs is tasked with assigning a fair value to all imports.

This is different from the free on board (FOB) price which is includes the cost of goods at the point of shipment; including cost of goods sold, loading charges, and export documentation

Buyers either agree to FOB Origin or FOB Destination pricing: FOB Origin means the sellers liability ends as soon the goods are loaded onto the transportation where FOB Destination means that sellers are liable until the goods are delivered.

By July 1, 2023, China’s official average import tariff rate will be 7.3 percent.

{Tariffs} There are a few general trends regarding tariffs:

  • Historically they were a very important source of revenue for governments, but after WWII they declined in importance- After WWII many government preferred to use nontariff barriers such as import quotas and export restraints- Developing countries (especially export reliant) tend to impose higher tariffs than developed countries

{Tariffs} When we do a theoretical analysis of tariffs on an economy, we will be doing \textbf{partial equilibrium} analysis. That is, assume that trade policies toward one sector can be understood well enough without going into detail about the policies effects on the rest of the economy.

If we think that the effects on the entire economy are critical to understand a trade policy, then we need to use \textbf{general equilibrium} analysis.

All of our models of trade (Ricardo, Heckscher-Ohlin, New Trade) were general equilibrium since investigated how impacts to one industry affected all other industries too.

{Tariffs} Let’s begin our theoretical analysis of tariffs:

  • Two countries: Home and Foreign- One competitive industry: Wheat

We expect that the supply and demand curves in each country will depend on the local currency price. Let’s assume that the exchange rate between currencies is not affected by whatever trade policy is undertaken (this is an example or partial equilibrium analysis), so we can quote prices in both markets in Home’s currency.

{Tariffs} Assume that the autarky price of wheat in Home is higher than in Foreign (you should be able to think of a few reasons why this could be the case).

When Home and Foreign trade, the price of wheat in Home will rise and the price of wheat in Foreign will fall until the price differences are gone.

We are using the same logic as in our classical trade models, but we will add an additional useful tool: Home’s \textbf{import demand curve} and Foreign’s \textbf{export supply curve}

{Tariffs} \begin{figure} \centering \includegraphics[scale=.6]{export_supply.pdf} \end{figure}

{Tariffs} If countries were not trading, then the equilibrium autarky price would simply be where domestic demand equals domestic supply.

If countries are trading, then the equilibrium world price is where world demand equals world supply… \begin{align*} \text{World Demand} &= \text{World Supply} \ \text{Home Demand}+\text{Foreign Demand} &= \text{Home Supply} + \text{Foreign Supply} \ \text{Home Demand}-\text{Home Supply} &= \text{Foreign Supply} - \text{Foreign Demand} \ \text{Home Import Demand} &= \text{Foreign Export Supply} \end{align*} …or where Home’s import demand equals Foreign’s export supply.

{Tariffs} \begin{figure} \centering \includegraphics[scale=.8]{import_export_equilibrium.pdf} \end{figure}

{Tariffs} We will consider two cases: \begin{enumerate}- Home is a \textit{small} open economy: Home’s trade policies have no effect on foreign export prices. We can think of this as the Foreign export supply curve being perfectly flat- Home is \textit{large} open economy: Home’s trade policies do have an effect on foreign export prices. Home faces an upward sloping export supply curve. \end{enumerate}

{Tariffs} From the point of view of someone shipping a good, a tariff is just like a cost of transportation: If Home imposes a specific tariff of $2 on every bushel of wheat imported, shippers will be unwilling to move the wheat unless the price differences between the two markets is at least $2.

Recall that we already investigated how transport costs could affect the pattern of trade in our classical models. We found that a large enough tariff will cause some goods to become non-traded because the difference between the import price and the domestic price is not larger than the transport cost.

Here, we are not looking at the pattern of trade, but instead the effects of a tariff on the specific industry.

{Tariffs in a Large Open Economy} \begin{figure} \includegraphics[scale=.6]{tariff_large.pdf} \centering \caption{A specific tariff of \(\tau\) raises the price of the imported good for Home while lowering the price in Foreign. The increase in price in Home, from \(P^W\) to \(P^{\tau}\) is less than the amount of the tariff.} \end{figure}

{Tariffs} If Home imposes a specific tariff of $2 on every bushel of wheat imported, shippers will be unwilling to move the wheat unless the price differences between the two markets is at least $2.

If no wheat is being shipped, however, there will be an excess demand for wheat in Home and excess supply in Foreign. Therefore, the price in Home will rise and the price in Foreign will fall.

The price in Home will rise and the price in Foreign will fall until the price difference is equal to the tariff \(\tau\).

{Tariffs} A specific import tariff \(\tau\) imposed by Home drives a wedge between the prices in the two markets:

  • a tariff raises the price in Home from \(P_W\) to \(P_T\)- a tariff lowers the price in Foreign to \(P_T-\tau\)

Home producers supply more at a higher price, while consumers demand less, so fewer imports are demanded. Foreign producers supply less at a lower price, while consumers demand more, so there is a smaller export supply.

{Tariffs} The tariff results in

  • lower volume of wheat traded \(Q_{\tau}<Q_W\)- a wedge in between the Home and Foreign price \(P_T-P^F = \tau\)

This analysis assumes that Home is an important part of the wheat industry, so it’s decision to impose a tariff impacted Foreign’s price. This is why the rise in Home’s price is not equal to the tariff \(\tau\).

If Home were a small player in the wheat industry, then it’s tariff will have little (approximately zero) effect on on Foreign’s export price. In this case, Home’s price rise is equal to the tariff \(\tau\).

{Effective Rate of Protection} The goal of a tariff is often to protect an import-competing industry, resulting in an increase in the domestic price of that good.

In practice, we want to measure the amount of protection a tariff affords an industry. One useful metric is \textit{the percentage of the price that would prevail under free trade.}

Example: An import tariff on sugar raised the domestic market price of sugar by 35 percent compared to the free trade price.

  • If the tariff is ad valorem, the tariff rate itself measures the amount of protection.- If the tariff is specific, dividing the tariff by the price net of the tariff gives an ad valorem equivalent

{Effective Rate of Protection} There are two problems with trying to calculate the rate of protection this way: \begin{enumerate}- The small-country assumption may not be a good approximation of reality, so part of the effect of a tariff will be to lower foreign export rather than raise domestic import prices.- Tariffs may have very different effects on different stages of production of a good \end{enumerate}

{Effective Rate of Protection} Consider the following example to see why tariffs may have different effects on different stages of production of a good:

  • Imagine that a car sells for $8,000 in the world market, and the parts out of which the car is made sell for $6,000- To encourage domestic car industry, Home levies a 25% tariff, allowing domestic car assemblers to charge $10,000 instead of $8,000

It is incorrect to say that car assemblers receive 25 percent protection.

  • Before the tariff, domestic assembly takes place only if it can be done for $2,000 (the different between $8,000 of a completed car and $6,000 cost of parts)- After the tariff, domestic assembly takes place even it costs as much as $4,000- The tariff provides assemblers with an effective rate of protection of 100 percent!

{Effective Rate of Protection}

  • Now suppose that Home imposes a 10% tariff on imported parts, raising the cost of parts for domestic assemblers from $6,000 to $6,600- Before the tariff, it would have been worth assembling a car locally if it could be done for $2,000- After the tariff, local assembly takes place only if it can be done for $1,400- The tariff on parts, provides protection to parts manufacturers, but -30% (600/2,000) protection to car assemblers

{Effective Rate of Protection} Similar reasoning leads economists to make complex calculations to measure the degree of effective protection provided by trade protection policies.

The effective rate of protection for an industry is formally

\[ \frac{V_T-V_W}{V_W} \]

where \(V_W\) is the value added to the industry at world prices; \(V_T\) is the value added in the presence of trade policies.

In our example of Home’s 25% import tariff on cars we have

\[ \frac{4,000-2,000}{2,000} = 100\% \]

{Effective Rate of Protection} Many industrial nations have a ``cascading” tariff structure with very low or zero tariffs on raw materials and higher and higher rates the greater is the degree of processing.

  • By making finished imports more expensive, countries encourage firms to process raw materials locally rather than importing finished products- Finished goods often have higher prices, so taxing them at higher rates generates more tariff revenue for the government- The rate of effective protection on a final commodity with imported inputs much greater than the nominal tariff rate would indicate

{Costs and Benefits of a Tariff} We observed that a tariff raises the price of a good in the importing country and lowers it in the exporting country. We can immediately say

  • Consumers lose in the importing country and gain in the exporting country- Producers gain in the importing country and lose in the exporting country- The government imposing the tariff gains revenue

{Costs and Benefits of a Tariff} In order to compare these costs and benefits, we need to quantify them using two additional concepts in economics: \begin{enumerate}- \textbf{Consumer surplus}: the amount a consumer gains from being able to purchase a good at a lower price than he would have been willing to pay- \textbf{Producer surplus}: the amount a producer gains from being able to sell its good at a higher price than he would have been willing to sell it for \end{enumerate}

{Gains from Free Trade} \begin{figure} \centering \includegraphics[scale=.4]{tp_22} \end{figure}

{Costs and Benefits of a Tariff} Now we have a way to measure the degree to which a tariff benefits producers and hurts consumers.

We can sum the costs and benefits to see whether a tariff benefits the nation or hurts the nation.

The net effect of a tariff on welfare is

\[ \text{consumer loss} - \text{producer gain} - \text{government revenue} \]

{Costs and Benefits of a Tariff} \begin{figure} \centering \includegraphics[scale=.8]{tariff_welfare_large.pdf} \end{figure}

{Costs and Benefits of a Tariff} The net effect of a tariff on welfare is \begin{align*} &\text{consumer loss} - \text{producer gain} - \text{government revenue} \ &(a+b+c+d)-a-(c+e)=b+d-e \end{align*}

  • Regions \(b\) and \(d\) represent \textbf{efficiency loss} that arises because a tariff distorts incentives to consume and produce- Regions \(c\) and \(e\) represent government revenue that arises from collecting tariffs- Part of the government revenue comes from \textbf{terms of trade gains}, given by region \(e\) that arises when the import tariff reduces the exporting country’s price- If this were a small country, then region \(e\) would disappear since there are no terms of trade effects

{Net Effect of an Import Tariff} \begin{figure} \centering \includegraphics[scale=.8]{tariff_welfare_large_net.pdf} \end{figure}

{Optimal Tariff} One argument for imposing tariffs is that a large country can manipulate its terms of trade favorably and increase national welfare.

It’s possible that region \(e\) is larger than the losses from regions \(b+d\).

You can show that for a sufficiently small tariff, the terms of trade benefits always outweigh the costs. However, as the tariff gets larger the losses become to large and social welfare is hurt.

{Trade Warfare} Retaliation against protectionist trade policies is a real concern.

Example: After Trump imposed punitive tariffs on China, China responded with more tariffs of its own.

Trade wars ruin so many gains achieved through free trade. So why do they happen?

{Trade Warfare} Suppose there are two countries: the United States and Japan.

Suppose that there are two policy choices: free trade or protection.

We can represent the strategic interaction of these countries in a game matrix. \begin{figure} \centering \includegraphics[scale=.6]{tp_17} \end{figure}

{Trade Warfare} The payoffs represent two assumptions:

  • Each country’s government would choose protection if it could take the other country’s policy as given (e.g. terms of trade argument, political interests, supporting domestic industry)- Both countries would be better off if the both engaged in free trade (there exist gains from trade)

This is an application of the \textbf{Prisoner’s dilemma}. Each country has a dominant strategy to protect itself from import competition, and the resulting Nash equilibrium is one where each country is worse off.

This is the rationale for why \textbf{trade agreements} (and the WTO) can be useful.

{Trade Warfare} Given our example of trade negotiations, it may seem puzzling why countries would ever agree to free trade at all!

We can justify this in the same game theoretic setting by appealing to the idea of repeated interactions: games are played several times and players understand this.

Let’s extend the Prisoner’s Dilemma to multiple periods and evaluate when free trade can be sustained as a subgame perfect Nash equilibrium.

  • Both countries have the same discount factor \(\beta \in (0,1)\)

  • Both countries adopt a \textbf{grim trigger strategy}: cooperation ends permanently if there is any defection

{Trade Warfare}

Payoff from always cooperating:

\[ V_C = \sum_{t=0}^\infty \beta^t \cdot 10 = \frac{10}{1 - \beta} \]

Payoff from defecting once and triggering permanent retaliation:

\[ V_D = 20 + \sum_{t=1}^\infty \beta^t \cdot (-5) = 20 + \frac{-5\beta}{1 - \beta} \]

Cooperation is sustainable if:

\[ \frac{10}{1 - \beta} \geq 20 - \frac{5\beta}{1 - \beta} \]

Which simplifies to:

\[ \beta \geq \frac{2}{5} \]

{Trade Warfare} If both countries care enough about the future, they will avoid a trade war and sustain free trade.

A grim trigger strategy may to to harsh and unrealistic. Instead, we could suppose the countries adopt a \textbf{forgiving trigger strategy}: punish defection with Protection for a fixed number of round \(T\), then return to Free Trade.

{Trade Warfare} Payoff from defecting once, facing \(T\) rounds of retaliation, then returning to cooperation: \begin{align*} V_D &= 20 + \sum_{t=1}^{T} \beta^t \cdot (-5) + \sum_{t=T+1}^\infty \beta^t \cdot 10 \ &=20 - 5 \left( \frac{1 - \beta^T}{1 - \beta} \right) + 10 \left( \frac{\beta^{T+1}}{1 - \beta} \right) \end{align*}

Cooperation is sustainable if:

\[ \frac{10}{1 - \beta} \geq 20 - 5 \left( \frac{1 - \beta^T}{1 - \beta} \right) + 10 \left( \frac{\beta{^T+1}}{1 - \beta} \right) \]

{Trade Warfare}

  • Longer punishment period \(T\) makes cooperation easier to sustain

  • Higher discount factor \(\beta\) also support cooperation

  • More realistic than grim trigger: After a violation (like a tariff hike), the injured party responds with temporary retaliatory tariffs, possibly escalating to the WTO or ending in negotiation. Once resolved, countries usually restore normal trade rather than ending cooperation forever.

{Trade Warfare} There are many other strategies you can consider:

  • Tit-for-Tat: Begin with cooperation, then do whatever your opponent did in the previous rounds

  • Tit-for-\(T\)-Tat: Begin with cooperation, then retaliate if your opponent defects \(T\) times in a row

  • Win-Stay, Lose-Shift: If you received a good payoff last round, repeat your action. Otherwise, switch your action.

  • Mixed strategy trigger: After defection, punish with some probability.

Which strategy is actually employed depends on many factors including: patience, risk of noise, reputation, communication, enforcement, number of players etc.

{Trade Warfare} Trade agreements and institutions (like the WTO) act as formal mechanisms to facilitate cooperation and reduce the need for harsh unilateral punishment.

{Measuring the cost of tariffs} The partial equilibrium analysis we investigated is also used by policy makers and academics to measure the real-world cost of tariffs.

{Measuring the cost of tariffs} World Integrated Trade Solution (WITS) provides a simulation tool called SMART which performs partial equilibrium analysis of trade policy changes at the industry level. (Have a look: https://wits.worldbank.org/wits/wits/witshelp/Welcome.htm and click on “SMART”). You can specify a tariff (addition or reduction) for a particular industry and it will estimate:

  • Trade creation effect

  • Trade diversion effect

  • Price effect effect

  • Tariff revenue

  • Welfare change

The software defaults to an infinitely elastic export supply curve, but you can change this assumption.

{Measuring the cost of tariffs} Amiti, Redding, and Weinstein (2019) use partial equilibrium analysis to measure the impact of the 2018 US trade war.

\textit{The deleterious impacts of the tariffs have been largely in line with what one might have predicted based on a simple supply and demand framework. We estimate the likely impact on U.S. consumers and find that by the end of 2018, import tariffs were costing U.S. consumers and the firms that import foreign goods an additional $3 billion per month in added tax costs and another $1.4 billion dollars per month in deadweight welfare (efficiency) losses. Tariffs have also changed the pricing behavior of U.S. producers by protecting them from foreign competition and enabling them to raise prices and markups.}

{Measuring the cost of tariffs} ARW (2019) first compute the 1 year change of a price index for a bundle of imports affected by the Trump 2018 tariffs $\( \hat{P}_t=\prod_{\omega \in \Omega}\hat{p}_{ijt}(\omega)^{s_{ij}(\omega)} \)\( where \)\hat{p}t=p_t/p{t-12}\( and \)s_{ij}\( is the logarithmic mean of import shares from country \)j\( in sector \)i\( among all HTS10 imports in categories affected by tariff wave \)\omega$.

(Note: This is the exact price index for CES demand system)

They also compute the same index but use total value of imports rather than unit values.

{Measuring the cost of tariffs}

- ARW (2019) use US Customs data which reports foreign export values and quantities at the HTS10 level
- Dividing export values by quantities gives unit values at a very disaggregated level (e.g. baseball gloves made in China)
- Multiplying unit values by the tariff rates from the U.S. International Trade Commission gives tariff inclusive prices

{Amiti, Redding, and Weinstein (2019)} To measure the welfare impact of the tariffs, ARW (2019) need to measure the degree to which foreign’s export supply curve is inelastic.

\textit{A necessary condition for the U.S. to gain from these tariffs in the classical model is that foreign exporters absorb some of the tariff costs so that they are not fully born by home consumers Thus, understanding how the price received by foreign exporters moves in response to a tariff increase is a first key step in understanding the welfare implications.}

{Amiti, Redding, and Weinstein (2019)} Run regression, \begin{align*} \hat{p}_t&=\beta_0+\beta_1 \hat{\tau}_t+\epsilon_t \ \hat{m}_t&=\beta_2+\beta_3 \hat{\tau}_t+\epsilon_t \end{align*} where \(\hat{\tau}=\ln(1+\tau_t)/(1+\tau_{t-12})\) and \(\hat{m}\) is the percentage change in import quantities. The estimated coefficient \(\beta_1\) is the impact of tariffs on the prices received by foreign exporters. The coefficient \(\beta_3\) is the import demand elasticity.

  • Column 1 suggests that export supply to the US is close to perfectly elastic

  • Column 2 suggests an elasticity of import demand of 1.299

{Amiti, Redding, and Weinstein (2019)} Multiplying the tariff changes by the estimated elasticity suggest that the 2018 U.S. tariffs reduced U.S. imports by about 54 percent, amounting to $136 billion.

{Amiti, Redding, and Weinstein (2019)} Also estimate the deadweight loss (regions b+d in our figure) associated with the tariff:

\[ DWL=1/2 p_1^* \tau (m_0-m_1)=1/2 (p_1^* m_1)\tau \frac{m_0-m_1}{m_1} \]

where \(p_1^* m_1\) is the value of imports after the tariff, \(\tau\) is the tariff rate, and \((m_0-m_1)/m_1\) is the percentage change in quantity of imports after the tariff. Using the regression results they compute

\[ DWL=-1/2 (p_1^* m_1)\tau \beta_3 \ln \left(\frac{1+\tau_t}{1+\tau_{t-12}} \right) \]

{Amiti, Redding, and Weinstein (2019)} ARW (2019) also discuss the fact the tariffs may reduce the variety of goods imported into the US, and construct a variety-adjusted price index which accounts for changes in the set of products over time. They also find that tariffs appear to be passed through completely in terms of domestic prices for common goods.

{Amiti, Redding, and Weinstein (2019)} Lastly, they consider the effect of import competition on markups of domestic firms. They use disaggregated data from the producer price index to get prices charged by domestic producers.

  • For the average firm that imports 15 percent of its variable costs, a 10 percent higher input tariff causes it to raise its own prices by 2.7 percent. Some of the input costs are not passed through because of endogenous markups.

  • For a typical sector in which a quarter of all domestic sales are by foreign firms, a ten percent tariff is associated with a 1.3 percent increase in domestic producer prices over 12 months

{Amiti, Redding, and Weinstein (2019)} Main findings of ARW (2019):

  • Cumulative deadweight welfare cost from 2018 U.S. tariffs were $6.9 billion with an additional cost of $12.3 billion to domestic consumers and producers in the form of tariff revenue

  • Tariffs caused about $136 billion in imports to be lost or redirected

  • U.S. tariffs were completely passed through to U.S. domestic prices, so that the entire incidence of tariffs fell on consumers and importers

  • U.S. producers responded to tariffs by raising their prices

{Trade policy uncertainty} One potentially important cost of tariffs, which is ignored in this study, is the effect of trade policy uncertainty on the decision of exporters to enter the world market.

Handley and Limao (2017) develop a dynamic model where firms must pay of a sunk entry cost and there exists an option value of waiting due to trade policy uncertainty. They show that, indeed, trade policy uncertainty deters investment by exporting firms and entry of firms into the export sector.

{The China Shock} What were the reasons for the US China trade war in 2018 and 2025?

Although political reasons were at play, let’s restrict attention to the economic impact that rising imports from China had on the US.

China’s accession to the WTO in 2001 signaled China’s growing role in the international goods market, especially the manufacturing sector.

{The China Shock} Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2013) measure how exposed regional labor markets in the US were to import competition from China

\[ \Delta IPW_{it}=\sum_{j}\frac{L_{ij,1990}}{L_{j,1990}}\frac{\Delta M_{jt}}{L_{it}} \]

where

  • \((L_{ij}/L_{j})\) is the share of region \(i\) in the US employment in industry \(j\)

  • \(\Delta M_{jt}\) is the observed change in US imports from China in industry \(j\) between the start and end of the period

  • \(L_{it}\) is the start of period employment (year \(t\)) in region \(i\)

The formula shows that a region is more exposed to Chinese import growth if it specializes (has a higher-than-average employment share) in industries where U.S. imports from China are rising. The import growth is scaled by the size of the region.

{The China Shock} \begin{figure} \includegraphics[scale=0.4]{ADH_12.PNG} \end{figure}

{The China Shock} In order to isolate the supply-driven China shock (control for U.S. domestic shocks), ADH (2013) use the following instrument for change in imports: Chinese exports to high-income countries (excluding the US).

The instrumental variable approach aims to identify the component of US import growth that is due to Chinese productivity and trade costs. The identifying assumption underlying this strategy is that the common within-industry component of rising Chinese imports to the United States and other high-income countries is due to China’s rising comparative advantage and falling trade costs.

{The China Shock} Main findings:

  • Rising Chinese imports negatively impacted workers who were exposed the most to import competition

  • Manufacturing workers suffered higher unemployment and discouraged workers

  • Non-manufacturing workers realized lower wages suggesting general equilibrium effects within a region
