Measuring Business Cycles#

Distinguishing Short-Run v. Long-Run#

We observe that real GDP tends to increase over time…

…but that this growth is interrupted frequently by boom and bust periods

If we were just statisticians we could do the following:

  • Take the time series for real GDP over a long time horizon

  • Use a statistical method (e.g. log-quadratic filtering or HP filtering) to separate the trend and cyclical component of the time series

\[ Y_t = Y_t^{\text{trend}}+Y_t^{\text{cycle}} \]

The trend component reflects the long-term trajectory of the economy while the cycle component reflects short term deviations around the trend.

If we were economists then we could distinguish between the long-run and short-run in the following way:

  • Long-run GDP, called potential GDP, represents the level of production at which resources are being fully and sustainably utilized.

  • Short-run GDP, called actual GDP, is the realized level of output taking into account any shocks that are hitting the economy.

When the economy is in a long-run equilibrium

\[ \text{Actual GDP}=\text{Potential GDP} \]

When the economy is pushed off it’s long-run equilibrium by shocks

\[ \text{Actual GDP} \neq \text{Potential GDP} \]

Our theory of long-run GDP comes from Solow/Romer/Equilibrium Unemployment. Our theory of long-run inflation comes from the Quantity Theory of Money and the Fisher equation. Now we want to develop a theory to explain short-run fluctuations of the economy away from its long-run trajectory.

In our model, we will describe short-run output in terms of percentage deviations of actual output from potential output

\[ \tilde{Y}_t = \frac{Y_t-\bar{Y}_t}{\bar{Y}_t} \]

where \(Y_t\) is actual output and \(\bar{Y}_t\) is the long-run level of output. Notice the bar notation indicating that potential output is determined outside this model. It is determined by our theories of the long-run! (Note: The Congressional Budget Office of the US uses a version of the Solow model to construct its measure of potential GDP).

Some data facts (based on the previous pictures) that our short-run model should match:

  • Short-run output follows a boom-bust cyclical pattern

  • High levels of unemployment accompany low short-run output

  • Low short-run output (recession) usually causes a fall in inflation

Two key ideas to explain why the economy fluctuates away from it’s long-run equilibrium.

  • The economy is being constantly hit by shocks. These economic shocks include changes in commodity prices, disruptions in financial markets, changes in military spending, natural disasters, pandemics etc.

  • Monetary and fiscal policy are used to affect output. The government (fiscal authority + monetary authority) has tools to respond to shocks. For example, central bank may lower interest rates to stimulate the economy after a bad recession. Or the government may send checks in the mail to everyone to stimulate spending during the pandemic.
